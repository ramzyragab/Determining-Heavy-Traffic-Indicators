Determining Heavy Traffic Indicators¶

1. Introduction¶

Heavy traffic is a growing problem in several countries around the world. It is problematic for different reasons. Heavy traffic has a negative impact on the environment as well as life quality for those who have to spend hours to commute between their homes and workplaces. Also, the economic costs of heavy traffic can be enormous, with several countries losing billions of dollars every year as a result. This research project aims to determine a few indicators of heavy traffic, and uses a dataset about westbound traffic on the I-94 Interstate highway connecting several regions in the northern parts of the United States.

The histogram above displays the distribution of traffic volume on the I-94 Interstate highway. What it exactly shows is the number of vehicles that pass the test station per hour. Note that one row in the dataset represents the different variables for one hour. The horizontal axis displays ten binned clusters of traffic volume, each bar representing an incremental increase of roughly 700. The vertical axis shows the frequency of the observed traffic volume.

We observe a skewed bimodal distribution of traffic volume. While the mean value of the traffic_volume column is 3,259.82 - see the descriptive statistics table below - the actual frequency of such traffic volumes is rather low. Especially the first and seventh bars in the histogram however, are remarkably higher. This implies that there have been many observations of traffic volumes that are either substantially lower or higher than the average traffic volume. In other words, the I-94 highway is regularly either (close to) empty, or quite busy. This observation is further supported by the descriptive statistics table below, which indicates that about 25 per cent of the time, there are 1,193 or less vehicles on the road. Also, about 25 per cent of the time, there are 4,933 or more* vehicles on the road, which is more than four times as much as in the lowest quartile.

An obvious first guess would be that this bimodal distribution signifies the difference between daytime and nighttime. This will be the next point of focus.

In the code above, we have split the data into a daytime dataset and a nighttime dataset. By doing so, we can analyze the daytime and nighttime traffic distributions separately. The daytime dataset contains all instances between 07:00 a.m. and 07:00 p.m. (or 19:00), and the nighttime dataset contains the instances between 07:00 p.m. (or 19:00) and 07:00 a.m.

The differences are striking. The most obvious observation is that the daytime data shows a normal distribution, while the nighttime data shows a right skewed distribution. More precisely, this means that the I-94 highway is relatively quiet. Descriptive statistics show us that 25 per cent of the time, there are only 486 or less vehicles on the road, while the fourth quartile only begins at 2,630 vehicles per hour. On average, only 1,655 vehicles pass by per hour during nighttime. To put that into perspective: at daytime, 25 per cent of the time, there are 4,021 or less vehicles on the road. This is almost ten times more than the 486 we've observed during nighttime. Furthermore, the mean value for the daytime dataset is three times more than the nighttime's, meaning that there are three times as many vehicles on the road during daytime than nighttime, on average. Very rarely do we see such high numbers during nighttime, which raises questions about the relevance of the nighttime data for our research project. With busy nights being an exemption, it might be a good idea to leave them out for the rest of the project. To further investigate the time factor, we will focus on months, days of the week and times of the day in the sections below.

Comparing the hourly traffic volume during working days and weekend days, we observe a striking difference. On working days, the morning and late afternoon rush hours see large spikes in traffic volume. After 6:00 p.m. (or 18:00), the traffic volume decreases sharply. Here too, commuting may account for a significant part of heavy traffic. The weekend chart shows a different pattern, with lighter traffic in the early morning and early evening, and higher traffic volumes during the day. One explanation could be that less people are working during the weekend. And those who are free may spend their time shopping, leisure, visiting friends and relatives, and other activities that often take place at daytime.

To conclude the analysis of time indicators, we can summarize the time effects as follows: First, at nighttime there is usually (very) light traffic on the I-94 Interstate highway, while there is a more normal distribution of traffic volume during daytime. Second, the highway has much lighter traffic during the months of January, February, July and December than through the rest of the year. Third, the average traffic volume is constantly high on working days, and decreases sharply during the weekend. Finally, two very high peaks of traffic volume are observed during working days - one around 7:00 in the morning, and the other around 16:00 (or 4:00 p.m.) in the afternoon. Contrary, on weekend days, traffic is light in the morning, and quite evenly distributed across the afternoon hours.

8. Weather Indicators¶

So far, our focus of this project has been on time indicators. We have analyzed the differences in traffic volume between daytime and nighttime, months, days of the week, and hours during working days and weekend days. The monthly chart showed a pattern of heavier traffic during warm months and lighter traffic during cold months, with July being the exception. Another possible indicator of heavy traffic is weather. The I-94 Interstate highway dataset provides us with a few useful variables about the weather. These are:

  • temp - the average hourly temperature in kelvin
  • rain_1h - the amount of rain that occurred in the hour, in millimetres
  • snow_1h - the amount of snow that occurred in the hour, in millimetres
  • clouds_all - the percentage of cloud cover
  • weather_main - short textual description of the current weather
  • weather_discription - a categorical, longer textual description of the current weather

The first four columns are all numeric columns. This enables us to examine their correlation values with traffic_volume. The output will be evaluated below.

9. Weather Types¶

While the four numeric weather columns contain valuable information, their practical use is limited for our research project. There are several weather related variables that are not captured by these four columns. For instance, the rain_1h and snow_1h columns do not tell us anything about how much rain or snow there has already been in the previous hours or days, nor do the four columns contain information about factors such as thunderstorms. Such factors may contribute to traffic congestion, so it is well worth investigating their impact on the traffic volume. We'll start by looking at the weather_main column, which gives a simple discription of the weather that occurred in each hour.

10. Conclusion¶

In this project we have aimed to identify indicators of heavy traffic. We've used a dataset compromised of westbound traffic data from the I-94 Interstate highway in the northern parts of the United States. We've found a binomal distribution of traffic volume, possibly caused by the very low traffic volume at nighttime. Daytime traffic volume showed a normal distribution, with peaks on working days around 07:00 and 16:00 and a more equal distribution of traffic volume on weekend days. Crucially, the data shows that overall traffic volume is much higher on working days than on weekend days, suggesting that commuting is a major cause of heavy traffic. We've also found that, except for July, a general pattern of higher traffic volumes in the warmer months (March-October) and lower traffic volumes in the colder months (November-February). However, weather indicators and weather types only show very low correlation to traffic volumes, with only shower snow and the combination of light rain and snow showing higher traffic volume. That being said, this project concludes with the finding that working days, rush hours and non-holiday months are probably the strongest indicators of heavy traffic.
